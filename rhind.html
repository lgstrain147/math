Method:

Phase 1
  • Find Q such Q multiplied by n is greater than 1 and less than or equal to 2.
  • Generally, multiply by 2/3 then successive halves (1/3,1/6,1/12,1/24) as in the case of 3,5,9,11,23,29 below
    • as n increases take 10 rather than 1/2 (ie. 2/3,1/3.1/30 or 2/3,1/3,1/6,1/60)
    • x = 1 (2/3 n) is a special case used when n % 3 = 0 and n >= 21
    • exceptions:
      • sometimes when n % 5 = 0, x = 1 (3 * n/5) and x = 1 2/3 (ie. 25,65,85)
      • sometimes when n % 7 = 0, x = 1 / (4 * n/7) and x = 1 1/2 1/4 (ie. 49,77)
      • sometimes skip 2/3 and start with 1/2 ie. 5,7,13
      • sometimes start with 1/10 instead of 2/3 (ie 15,31,61,67,71 and 91
      • 43: x = 1/42
      • 59: x = 1/36 (1/3 (or 2/3 and then 1/2 [11]) rather than 1/2 of 1/12)
      • 97: x = 1/56 (successive halves of 1/7 [12])/li>
      • 101: x = 1/101
        • note: this method could be used for all cases
Phase 2

Account for the remainder:

  • it can be represented multiple cobminations of reciprocal fractions
    • generally, the largest denominator as small as possible is used
  • mutiply by the inverses of the remainder and invert

Quantity Problems (Divison by 1 and a fraction)

Approach:

What Q * (1 1/D) = R?

  1. Assume answer: is the inverse of the fraction (D)
  2. Perform computation: multiply by the divisor (1 1/D)
  3. Adjust assumption by ratio of desired result to result of computation based on assumption:
    • Determine how many time the result of step 2 must be multiplied by to get the dividend(R)
    • Multiply the result of step 3 by D
  4. Verify by performing computation with adjusted assumption: prove the result of step 4 * (1 1/7) = R

This technique is known as "false position." [28]
